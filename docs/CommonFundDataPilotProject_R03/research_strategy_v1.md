# Research Strategy

## 1. Significance

### 1.1 The Post-GWAS Translational Bottleneck

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have catalogued tens of thousands of robust variant-trait associations across hundreds of human diseases and quantitative traits, establishing the genetic architecture of common complex conditions with unprecedented resolution (Claussnitzer et al., 2020; Tam et al., 2019). Despite this achievement, the causal genes, tissue-specific regulatory mechanisms, and druggable protein targets underlying the majority of these associations remain unresolved. More than 90% of GWAS signals reside in non-coding genomic regions, where causal variants perturb gene regulation through mechanisms that require multi-modal functional evidence to decipher (Gallagher and Chen-Plotkin, 2018; Cannon and Mohlke, 2018). The consequence is a pronounced gap between statistical discovery and biological understanding: thousands of associated loci await functional interpretation, and the translational potential of this genetic evidence for drug target identification and biomarker discovery remains largely unrealized.

Bridging this gap requires a defined translational inference chain traversing five sequential investigating stages: (1) assignment of GWAS signals to candidate target genes through multi-modal regulatory evidence; (2) quantification of variant effects on tissue-specific and cell-type-specific gene expression; (3) evaluation of the pharmacological tractability of implicated gene products; (4) prediction of transcriptomic responses to pharmacological perturbation of candidate targets; and (5) projection of transcriptomic signals onto proteomic changes and identification of protein biomarkers for prognosis and treatment response. Each of these stages requires distinct computational methods, ontological frameworks, and empirical datasets. No currently available tool covers all five stages within a single automated workflow (Cano-Gamez and Trynka, 2020; Falola et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2025).

### 1.2 Making full use of NIH Common Fund Program Data

The NIH Common Fund has made a transformative and sustained investment in precisely the data modalities required to execute this translational chain. Twenty Common Fund programs collectively provide chromatin architecture maps (4D Nucleome, 4DN), tissue- and cell-type-resolved single-cell atlases (Human BioMolecular Atlas Program, HuBMAP; Cellular Senescence Network, SenNet), multi-tissue cis-regulatory variant catalogs (Genotype-Tissue Expression, GTEx), controlled-perturbation multi-omic time series (Molecular Transducers of Physical Activity in Humans, MoTrPAC), systematic compound and genetic perturbation signatures (Library of Integrated Network-based Cellular Signatures, LINCS), target pharmacology annotations across the druggable genome (Illuminating the Druggable Genome, IDG), longitudinal clinical biomarker cohorts (Acute to Chronic Pain Signatures, A2CPS; Integrated Human Microbiome Project, iHMP), ancestry-diverse genomic resources (H3Africa), knockout phenotype atlases (Knockout Mouse Phenotyping Program, KOMP2), and glycoproteomic reference data (GlyGen), among others.

Despite this investment, these resources remain functionally siloed. Each program serves a distinct user community, operates through a separate data portal with a distinct access model, and employs program-specific identifier systems and data formats. Integrating these resources into a coherent translational analysis requires expert knowledge of program-specific APIs, consent-based access controls, identifier harmonization protocols, and computational workflows that are themselves specialized. For example, a researchers who possess genetic expertise often lack the capacity to systematically leverage regulatory, perturbation, and clinical data from the full Common Fund datasets, and vice versa. That is to say, the translational potential of twenty programs remains piecemeal.

### 1.3 Absence of Automated End-to-End Infrastructure

Several computational systems address individual stages of post-GWAS analysis. Fine-mapping tools (SuSiE, FINEMAP), colocalization methods (coloc, ecaviar), and transcriptome-wide association study (TWAS) frameworks (S-PrediXcan, FUSION, UTMOST) have substantially improved single-stage analyses. Databases such as Open Targets Genetics aggregate evidence across multiple data types for variant-to-gene prioritization. Tools such as MRAgent automate Mendelian randomization for causal inference between exposures and outcomes. LINCS connectivity analysis pipelines support compound-disease signature matching. However, no system currently orchestrates all five translational stages within a single automated infrastructure, passes calibrated uncertainty estimates across stage boundaries, manages controlled-access data authorization, or presents results in a unified knowledge graph enabling multi-hop evidence queries from genetic variant to therapeutic target.

Recent advances in general-purpose autonomous research agent frameworks, including LLM-orchestrated multi-stage pipelines built on orchestration layers such as LangGraph, might appear to render domain-specific infrastructure unnecessary. Such frameworks can invoke bioinformatics tools as subprocesses and relay outputs between stages, but adaptation to post-GWAS translational analysis fails at three barriers that are not resolvable through prompt engineering or tool specification alone. First, statistical methods such as SuSiE, FINEMAP, and coloc require dataset-specific parameterization that a general agent cannot reliably infer from context; misconfigured parameters produce numerically plausible outputs that are statistically invalid, and the failure is silent because malformed results are structurally indistinguishable from correct ones. Second, multiple data resources required for complete post-GWAS analysis require institutional authorization through dbGaP, the European Genome-phenome Archive, or platform-specific access systems; a general agent framework lacks architecture for pre-execution authorization checking, so access gaps surface as mid-run retrieval failures rather than pre-flight flags, corrupting partially completed session states and necessitating full pipeline restart. Third, continuous probabilistic quantities — posterior inclusion probabilities, colocalization H4 statistics, TWAS Z-scores — must be passed between stages as numerical values embedded in a typed persistent state object; text-serialized inter-agent communication collapses these gradients, and downstream stages that treat a summarized high-confidence call equivalently regardless of whether it carries a 0.62 or 0.89 posterior amplify upstream uncertainty rather than propagating it. These barriers require architectural solutions, not configuration choices.

The absence of infrastructure addressing all three barriers imposes direct costs. Manual integration across five analytical stages is prohibitively time-intensive, with expert-driven analyses requiring weeks to months per locus. Uncertainty is not propagated systematically, and a downstream stage may treat an upstream assignment as definitive when it carries substantial uncertainty, amplifying error without accounting for it. Reproducibility is compromised when analyses are assembled from heterogeneous tools without shared configuration management, provenance tracking, or version control.

### A.4 Potential Impact

An automated, end-to-end post-GWAS translational pipeline that unifies twenty NIH Common Fund datasets would substantially compress the timeline from genetic discovery to therapeutic hypothesis, democratize access to cross-program evidence integration for researchers without multi-domain computational expertise, and establish a reusable infrastructure that enhances the translational return on the NIH Common Fund portfolio. For complex diseases where multiple GWAS loci await translational resolution, including type 2 diabetes, lipid metabolic disorders, inflammatory bowel disease, and chronic pain, such a system would accelerate the identification of druggable targets and prognostic biomarkers at a scale and reproducibility currently beyond the reach of conventional expert-driven workflows.

## B. Innovation

### B.1 First End-to-End Automated Post-GWAS Translational Pipeline

**AgentGWAS** represents the first computational system to orchestrate a complete five-stage post-GWAS translational pipeline, traversing variant-to-gene resolution, variant-to-transcriptome propagation, druggability assessment, in-silico perturbation simulation, and transcriptome-to-proteome biomarker projection within a single large language model (LLM)-orchestrated framework. Prior systems address individual stages in isolation. KGWAS improves statistical detection through knowledge-graph-enhanced GWAS methods but does not address translational stages. MRAgent automates Mendelian randomization for a single causal inference step. BioAgents supports bioinformatics pipeline construction but is general-purpose and lacks the domain-specific translational chain and dataset integration that define AgentGWAS. No existing system spans all five stages or integrates data from twenty NIH Common Fund programs within a single orchestrated workflow.

The argument that a capable general autonomous research agent framework could be adapted to this purpose through appropriate tool specification and prompt engineering does not hold at the level of domain-specific failure modes. Four classes of failure are latent in any general-framework adaptation attempt; each corresponds to a specific architectural choice in AgentGWAS.

**Silent statistical misconfiguration.** The statistical tools at Stages 1 and 2 require parameter configurations that vary with study ancestry, sample size, trait genetic architecture, and tissue context. SuSiE requires a linkage disequilibrium reference matrix matched to the study population; mismatched ancestry yields posterior inclusion probabilities that are numerically plausible but statistically invalid. Coloc requires prior effect-size specifications calibrated to trait variance explained; default priors produce well-formed but misleading H4 posterior probabilities for high-polygenicity traits. S-PrediXcan requires expression prediction models built from tissue-matched training data; cross-tissue model substitution produces Z-scores with inflated type I error. Critically, all three failure modes produce outputs that are structurally indistinguishable from correctly parameterized results. A general LLM orchestrator without domain-specific validation logic cannot detect them, and the errors propagate downstream without triggering any exception or warning. AgentGWAS resolves this by implementing all statistical computations as Nextflow workflow processes that enforce typed input schemas, validate parameter configurations against dataset-specific constraints at runtime, and emit result objects that are validated against expected output ranges before state update. LLM reasoning is architecturally isolated from statistical computation.

**Pre-execution authorization failure.** Multiple NIH Common Fund datasets required for complete post-GWAS analysis, including GTEx protected-access data, H3Africa genomic resources, the iHMP cohorts, and the Gabriella Miller Kids First program, require institutional authorization through dbGaP, the European Genome-phenome Archive, AnVIL, or BioData Catalyst. A general agent framework has no architecture for pre-execution authorization checking. Authorization failures surface as mid-run data retrieval errors that corrupt partially completed session states and necessitate full pipeline restart. Adding authorization logic as a tool call does not resolve this failure mode because the cost of discovering an authorization gap is borne after compute resources have been allocated and upstream stages have completed. AgentGWAS resolves this through an access governance module that consults an authorization manifest at plan generation time, before any computation is dispatched, and surfaces all controlled-access requirements as pre-flight flags in the session plan presented to the investigator for review.

**Probabilistic state collapse.** The five-stage translational chain requires that posterior inclusion probabilities, H4 colocalization statistics, TWAS Z-scores, and connectivity tau statistics be propagated between stages as continuous numerical quantities. General agent frameworks serialize inter-agent communication as text. Continuous posteriors summarized as natural language lose the gradient structure that downstream Bayesian prioritization requires. A locus variant assigned a posterior inclusion probability of 0.62 and a variant assigned 0.89 may both be described in a text summary as strongly supported, but the distinction determines whether the locus proceeds to colocalization analysis or is returned to Stage 1 for supplementary evidence retrieval. Information loss at each stage boundary compounds across five stages, and reconstructing calibrated distributions from textual summaries depends on consistent LLM summarization behavior that cannot be structurally guaranteed. AgentGWAS resolves this through the PipelineState Pydantic object, which is passed between stages as a structured record with typed fields for all probabilistic quantities, enforces schema validation at every stage transition, and triggers conditional routing when evidence thresholds are not met.

**Translational chain underdetermination.** General agent frameworks are task-agnostic; the translational inference logic must be specified externally, and the specification must be correct. The five-stage chain traversing variant credible sets, tissue-specific eQTL colocalization, druggability classification, perturbation connectivity scoring, and proteomic biomarker projection represents validated domain knowledge encoding established post-GWAS methodology. Specifying this chain at the level of scientific validity required for benchmark recovery — correctly configuring the dependency structure between stages, identifying which datasets are primary versus supplementary at each stage, defining the uncertainty propagation rules at each transition — is equivalent in effort and expertise to building a domain-specific system. A general framework adapted to this purpose by an expert who encodes all of this logic has become a domain-specific system. AgentGWAS makes this encoding explicit, version-controlled, validated against benchmark loci with known ground truth, and reproducible across investigators and institutions.

### B.2 Innovation 1: Calibrated Uncertainty Propagation Across the Translational Chain

AgentGWAS introduces a formally specified uncertainty propagation architecture in which each pipeline stage emits calibrated posterior probability estimates that downstream stages must consume as continuous quantities rather than discretized binary calls. A typed, persistent state object is passed between stages recording evidence-weighted scores at every transition: posterior inclusion probabilities from fine-mapping at Stage 1; colocalization H4 posterior probabilities and TWAS Z-scores at Stage 2; Target Development Level classifications and chemical tractability scores at Stage 3; LINCS connectivity tau statistics and p-values at Stage 4; and hazard ratios with confidence intervals and cohort identifiers at Stage 5. Conditional routing within the LangGraph orchestration framework allows the system to back-propagate evidence insufficiency: when a stage emits an uncertainty flag, the graph returns to an earlier stage to request additional data modalities or refined cell-context information before proceeding. No existing post-GWAS system propagates continuous posteriors across a multi-stage translational chain, and no existing multi-agent biomedical system implements uncertainty-conditional routing of this form.

### B.3 Innovation 2: Mandatory Human-in-the-Loop Plan Review as Architectural Guarantee

A second fundamental innovation is the implementation of mandatory investigator oversight as a first-class architectural guarantee rather than an optional courtesy feature. Before any computational task is dispatched, AgentGWAS presents a structured, human-readable session plan to the investigator as a rendered markdown document specifying the proposed stages, datasets, statistical parameters, access authorizations, expected outputs, and estimated compute hours. Execution proceeds only upon explicit investigator approval through a LangGraph interrupt node that cannot be bypassed programmatically. The investigator may approve the plan, submit an edited version for re-parsing, or reject it entirely and submit a revised query. This design is distinguished from existing autonomous research agents (The AI Scientist, Robin, AutoResearchClaw in its initial release, Coscientist) that operate with minimal human oversight by default, and from systems such as GRASP that incorporate human feedback without implementing it as a mandatory, non-bypassable interrupt. The mandatory review preserves investigator responsibility for analytical intent, flags controlled-access authorization gaps before execution begins, and maintains compliance with regulatory requirements for human genomic data interpretation.

### B.4 Innovation 3: Unified Knowledge Graph for Multi-Hop Variant-to-Biomarker Evidence Queries

The third principal innovation is a property graph knowledge representation spanning the complete variant-to-biomarker evidence chain. Eleven typed node categories (Variant, CredibleSet, Gene, Tissue, CellType, Drug, Compound, Protein, Phenotype, Pathway, Microbiome) are connected by seven typed edge categories (MAPS_TO, COLOCALIZES_WITH, REGULATES, TARGETS, PERTURBS, PREDICTS_BIOMARKER, ASSOCIATES_WITH), each carrying calibrated uncertainty scores as edge properties. All intermediate results from all five stages are written to this graph, enabling multi-hop traversal queries that span the full variant-to-biomarker evidence chain across a single session and across multiple sessions. This design is distinguished from KGWAS, which maintains a large knowledge graph (11 million links) restricted to variant-gene relationships, and from GRASP, which employs graph reasoning for systems pharmacology without spanning the complete translational chain. The AgentGWAS knowledge graph is the first to unify all five translational layers with typed uncertainty edges within a single queryable substrate.

### B.5 Integration of Twenty NIH Common Fund Datasets Within a Single Access-Governed Framework

AgentGWAS provides dedicated data adapters, API clients, and access-manifest entries for twenty NIH Common Fund programs, constituting the largest dataset integration scope reported for any post-GWAS translational system. Multiple programs within this portfolio require controlled-access authorization through dbGaP, the European Genome-phenome Archive, the AnVIL platform, or BioData Catalyst. The access governance module maintains an authorization registry consulted before every data retrieval call; any dataset required by the proposed plan but not yet authorized is surfaced as an access flag in the session plan before execution begins, ensuring that controlled-access requirements are addressed proactively rather than discovered as mid-run failures.

## C. Approach

### C.1 Conceptual Framework

The central hypothesis of this project is that an LLM-orchestrated multi-agent framework can integrate heterogeneous NIH Common Fund datasets to recover validated causal genes and approved therapeutic targets at benchmark GWAS loci with greater reproducibility than single-program expert-driven analyses and at substantially compressed analysis timelines relative to conventional manual workflows. The objective is to develop, benchmark, and publicly release AgentGWAS, a modular agentic pipeline orchestrating the five-stage post-GWAS workflow described below. Validation will employ benchmark loci for type 2 diabetes (TCF7L2, SLC30A8) and lipid metabolic traits (PCSK9, APOC3), using recovery of known causal genes and identification of approved therapeutic targets as primary performance metrics.

The analytical pipeline traverses five interlocking stages organized as a directed acyclic graph. Stage 1 (variant-to-gene resolution) executes first and emits a gene-level credible set as input to both Stage 2 (variant-to-transcriptome propagation) and Stage 3 (druggability assessment), which proceed concurrently. Stages 2 and 3 feed their outputs jointly into Stage 4 (in-silico perturbation simulation), which in turn provides input to Stage 5 (transcriptome-to-proteome projection and biomarker discovery). This topology reflects the natural dependency structure of translational inference and enables parallel execution of independent stages to minimize wall-clock analysis time.

### C.2 Stage 1: Variant-to-Gene Resolution

**Objective.** Assign GWAS lead variants and their credible-set members to putative target genes by integrating chromatin conformation, regulatory element annotations, chromatin accessibility, and context-specific gene expression.

**Methods.** Fine-mapping will be performed using SuSiE (sum of single effects regression) and FINEMAP applied to GWAS summary statistics with in-sample or reference linkage disequilibrium matrices, generating posterior inclusion probabilities for each variant in the locus credible set. Credible-set variants will be mapped onto chromatin contact domains and enhancer-promoter loops from 4DN Hi-C, Micro-C, HiChIP, and PLAC-seq assays accessed through the 4DN Data Portal REST API. Variants will be overlapped with active regulatory element signatures derived from histone-modification ChIP-seq (H3K27ac, H3K4me1, H3K4me3) and chromatin accessibility measured by ATAC-seq and DNase-seq retrieved from HuBMAP and SenNet single-cell chromatin atlases. Cell-type-specific gene expression from GTEx bulk tissue data and HuBMAP single-cell atlases will provide tissue-context weighting for candidate gene prioritization. Multi-modal evidence scores will be aggregated using Activity-by-Contact (ABC) enhancer-gene modeling and locus-to-gene machine learning logic to produce a gene-level posterior probability for each candidate gene at each locus.

**Applicable datasets.** 4DN (primary: chromatin conformation and enhancer-promoter loops), HuBMAP (primary: single-cell chromatin accessibility and spatial regulatory maps), SenNet (supporting: senescence-state chromatin assays), GTEx (supporting: bulk tissue expression baselines), LINCS (supporting: cell-line-specific transcriptional baselines for cross-referencing).

**Expected output.** A fine-mapped credible-set-to-gene assignment table recording, for each locus, the credible-set variant list with posterior inclusion probabilities, the candidate gene list with multi-modal evidence scores, and the tissue and cell-type context supporting each assignment.

### C.3 Stage 2: Variant-to-Transcriptome Propagation

**Objective.** Quantify the causal effect of genetic variation on the transcriptome with tissue and cell-type specificity, using GTEx as the canonical ground-truth resource for cis-regulatory variant effects.

**Methods.** Colocalization of GWAS and cis-eQTL signals will be performed using coloc (posterior probability of shared causal variant, H4 statistic) and ecaviar across GTEx tissues relevant to the disease of interest, with SMR/HEIDI applied to test for pleiotropy. TWAS analyses will leverage tissue-specific expression prediction models from S-PrediXcan and FUSION to identify genes whose genetically predicted expression is associated with the trait at transcriptome-wide significance. Mendelian randomization using TwoSampleMR with cis-eQTL instruments will be applied to infer directional causal effects of transcript abundance on downstream phenotypes. Where available, single-cell eQTL data from HuBMAP will be integrated to refine the cell type of action for colocalized signals. Population-aware analyses using H3Africa genomic resources will be performed for signals with distinct allele-frequency and linkage-disequilibrium structure in non-European ancestries. For pediatric and rare-disease contexts, germline and tumor transcriptome data from the Gabriella Miller Kids First program and rare-variant expression anchors from the Undiagnosed Diseases Network will be consulted.

**Applicable datasets.** GTEx (primary: tissue cis-eQTL and sQTL catalogs, allele-specific expression, fine-mapped regulatory variants), MoTrPAC (supporting: exercise-perturbed multi-tissue transcriptomics extending GTEx with dynamic perturbation states), HuBMAP (supporting: single-cell transcriptomic references for cell-type attribution), SenNet (supporting: cell-state-stratified transcriptomes for aging-relevant contexts), H3Africa (supporting: African-ancestry genotype and transcriptomic resources), Kids First (supporting: pediatric transcriptomes), UDN (supporting: rare-disease transcriptomic anchors), SMaHT (supporting: somatic mosaicism effects in late-onset disease).

**External supplementation.** For pancreatic islet-specific analyses required by type 2 diabetes benchmark loci (TCF7L2, SLC30A8), external islet eQTL catalogs from the InsPIRE consortium, the T2D Knowledge Portal, and the Gaulton group islet open-chromatin and eQTL datasets will be pre-declared in the access governance manifest and integrated as supplementary data sources. This gap is a named, bounded dependency that does not alter the stage architecture.

**Expected output.** Tissue-stratified variant-to-transcript effect estimates including colocalization H4 posterior probabilities, TWAS association statistics, and Mendelian randomization point estimates and confidence intervals for each candidate gene-tissue pair.

### C.4 Stage 3: Druggability Assessment

**Objective.** Classify genes prioritized by Stages 1 and 2 according to the tractability of their encoded proteins as therapeutic targets, distinguishing clinically validated targets, emerging targets, and targets for which only preliminary chemical or biological evidence exists.

**Methods.** Candidate genes will be cross-referenced against Target Development Level (TDL) categories in the IDG TCRD database accessed through the Pharos GraphQL API: Tclin (approved drug mechanism), Tchem (active chemical probe), Tbio (biologically studied but without chemical evidence), and Tdark (minimally studied). Chemical probe availability, compound-target interaction data, and clinical trial history will be retrieved from Pharos and supplemented with ChEMBL compound activity data. Tissue- and cell-type-specific expression from GTEx will be used to assess on-target adverse effect exposure across non-primary tissues. Structural druggability evidence will be retrieved from PDB entries referenced in TCRD. Mammalian loss-of-function phenotypes from KOMP2/IMPC will be consulted to anticipate the functional consequences of pharmacological target modulation. Glycan and glycoprotein annotations from GlyGen will be incorporated for targets whose druggability is conditioned on post-translational modification state.

**Applicable datasets.** IDG (primary: TDL annotation, chemical probes, target evidence), LINCS (supporting: chemical perturbation signatures connecting compounds to targets), KOMP2 (supporting: mammalian loss-of-function phenotypes), GTEx (supporting: expression baselines for adverse effect exposure estimation), GlyGen (supporting: glycoprotein annotations for modification-dependent druggability).

**Expected output.** A ranked list of druggable candidate targets annotated by TDL classification, chemical tractability score, available probe and drug inventory, tissue-level expression exposure profile, and relevant murine loss-of-function phenotypes.

### C.5 Stage 4: In-Silico Perturbation Simulation

**Objective.** Predict the transcriptomic response to pharmacological perturbation of prioritized druggable targets, enabling selection of candidate compounds whose perturbation signatures reverse or otherwise modulate disease-associated expression programs.

**Methods.** Disease gene expression signatures will be constructed from case-control transcriptomic contrasts in disease-relevant tissues or from variant-weighted imputed expression vectors derived from Stage 2 TWAS results. These signatures will be scored against the LINCS L1000 compendium of approximately 1.5 million compound- and genetic-perturbation profiles using connectivity tau statistics, with permutation-based empirical p-value estimation. Cell-context matching between the query disease signature and the LINCS reference cell-line space will be performed using cosine similarity of basal expression profiles, with uncertainty quantification reflecting the degree of cell-context mismatch. Target-level evidence will be aggregated across all compounds sharing a nominal target to identify convergent perturbation evidence. Where available, MoTrPAC exercise-perturbation signatures will provide orthogonal physiological context for therapeutic hypotheses involving metabolic adaptation mechanisms. SenNet-associated senolytic and senomorphic perturbation contexts will be used for aging-related disease analyses. Bridge2AI AI-ready multi-omic datasets will be used for training and benchmarking predictive perturbation response models where cell-line representation is insufficient.

**Applicable datasets.** LINCS (primary: L1000, P100, Cell Painting perturbation compendium), IDG (supporting: compound-to-target mappings), Bridge2AI (supporting: AI-ready multi-omic datasets for predictive modeling), MoTrPAC (supporting: physiological perturbation context), SenNet (supporting: senolytic perturbation contexts).

**Expected output.** Compound prioritization reports specifying, for each prioritized target, the top connectivity-scored compounds, their connectivity tau statistics and empirical p-values, the cell-context match score, and a mechanistic hypothesis linking compound action to the disease expression program.

### C.6 Stage 5: Transcriptome-to-Proteome Projection and Biomarker Discovery

**Objective.** Map transcriptomic changes arising from Stage 4 perturbation analyses onto proteomic changes and identify protein species that function as biomarkers of disease prognosis or predictors of treatment response.

**Methods.** Harmonized transcript-protein pairing will be performed using matched bulk, single-cell, or spatial transcriptomic and proteomic datasets from MoTrPAC (matched tissue transcriptomics, proteomics, phosphoproteomics, acetylomics, and ubiquitylomics across controlled longitudinal perturbation) and A2CPS (longitudinal multi-omic cohort explicitly designed for treatment-outcome prediction in the acute-to-chronic pain transition). Post-transcriptional discordance between transcript and protein abundance will be modeled using extracellular RNA species from the ExRNA Communication program as candidate regulatory intermediaries. Survival analysis and treatment-response modeling will be performed against longitudinal clinical phenotypes from the iHMP cohorts (inflammatory bowel disease, type 2 diabetes, and pregnancy) to nominate prognostic and predictive protein candidates. Biofluid accessibility (plasma, serum, urine, cerebrospinal fluid, saliva) will be assessed for clinical translation feasibility using proteomic atlases supplemented with GlyGen glycoproteomic annotations. Metabolomic companion evidence from Metabolomics Workbench will be integrated to contextualize proteomic signals within broader metabolic programs.

**Applicable datasets.** MoTrPAC (primary: matched transcriptomics and proteomics with longitudinal sampling), A2CPS (primary: longitudinal multi-omic biomarker cohort for treatment-outcome prediction), iHMP (primary: host proteomic, metabolomic, and transcriptomic series with clinical outcomes), ExRNA (supporting: biofluid-accessible RNA species), GlyGen (supporting: glycoproteomic biomarker annotations), Metabolomics Workbench (supporting: metabolomic companion evidence), Bridge2AI (supporting: AI-ready datasets for multimodal prognostic modeling), Kids First and UDN (supporting: clinical-context transcriptomic and phenotypic anchors for pediatric and rare-disease validation).

**Expected output.** A curated ranked list of protein biomarker candidates with evidence for biofluid accessibility, prognostic utility indexed by hazard ratio and cohort attribution, treatment-response predictivity, and an assessment of the validation plan required for clinical translation.

### C.7 Software Architecture

AgentGWAS is implemented as a six-layer software system. From the user-facing surface downward, these layers are: the graphical user interface, the planner agent, the session and task management layer, the LangGraph orchestration layer, the bioinformatics compute layer, and the persistence layer comprising the knowledge graph, artifact store, and audit log.

**GUI layer.** An open-source deployment provides a multi-page Streamlit application supporting natural-language query submission, session plan review with approve/edit/reject controls, live stage progress monitoring via a streaming agent thought interface, and results browsing with an interactive knowledge graph visualization built using pyvis. A production AWS deployment provides equivalent functionality through a React single-page application backed by a FastAPI server with WebSocket-based real-time event streaming, suitable for multi-user concurrent deployments.

**Planner agent layer.** The planner agent is a dedicated LLM agent that accepts free-text investigator queries and produces a structured, Pydantic-validated SessionPlan object specifying the trait under investigation, GWAS source, genome build, stages to execute, datasets per stage, statistical parameters, access flags, expected outputs, and estimated compute hours. The plan is written to a human-readable session_plan.md file for investigator review. The access governance module cross-references every dataset listed in the plan against the access manifest before the plan is presented for review, surfacing all controlled-access authorization gaps as flagged items.

**Session and task management layer.** Sessions are identified by UUID and persisted in SQLite (open-source) or Amazon DynamoDB (AWS). Upon plan approval, the task dispatcher converts the SessionPlan into typed task messages enqueued through Celery and Redis (open-source) or Amazon SQS (AWS). Stage workers consume these messages and instantiate the corresponding LangGraph stage subgraphs. Stages with no inter-stage dependency execute concurrently: Stages 2 and 3 are dispatched in parallel upon Stage 1 completion; Stage 4 is dispatched when both Stage 2 and Stage 3 have written their outputs; Stage 5 is dispatched upon Stage 4 completion.

**LangGraph orchestration layer.** Each stage is implemented as a LangGraph StateGraph subgraph with a single entry node and exit node. A shared PipelineState Pydantic object is passed between stages recording all intermediate evidence structures. Uncertainty flags trigger conditional edges that route the orchestration graph back to earlier stages for supplementary evidence retrieval. Stage-specific agents bind an LLM (Claude via Anthropic API or Amazon Bedrock), a stage-appropriate system prompt, and a tool registry drawn from the tools directory. Tool calls to computationally intensive bioinformatics operations are dispatched as subprocess invocations to Nextflow workflows.

**Bioinformatics compute layer.** Statistically intensive, deterministic operations (SuSiE fine-mapping, FINEMAP, coloc, ecaviar, S-PrediXcan, Activity-by-Contact scoring, LINCS connectivity analysis) are implemented as Nextflow workflow processes executing within containerized environments pinned to specific image digests. Containers package the Bioconductor R environment (susieR, coloc, TwoSampleMR), the Python ML environment (predixcan, pybedtools, cyvcf2), and the LINCS analysis environment (cmapPy, clue.io REST client). Nextflow jobs are submitted to local HPC clusters via SLURM or to AWS Batch, with configuration toggled through the Nextflow profile system.

**Persistence layer.** The knowledge graph is implemented using Neo4j (open-source deployment) or Amazon Neptune (AWS deployment) with the schema described in section B.4. Identifier normalization is performed by a harmonizer component that resolves all entities to canonical ontology identifiers (dbSNP rsIDs for variants, Ensembl gene IDs, UniProt accessions for proteins, ChEMBL and PubChem CIDs for compounds, HPO and MONDO terms for phenotypes, Uberon for tissues, Cell Ontology for cell types, NCBI Taxonomy for microbiome entities) before any write operation. All pipeline runs are recorded with W3C PROV-compatible provenance logs capturing tool versions, model identifiers, parameter configurations, random seeds, and intermediate artifact hashes. Intermediate results are deposited to an S3 bucket or local artifact directory accessible through a consistent artifact store interface.

### C.8 Validation Strategy and Benchmark Loci

**Benchmark locus selection.** Four loci were selected as primary performance benchmarks against five explicit criteria: (1) definitive causal gene identity established by functional evidence; (2) approved therapeutic ground truth permitting unambiguous evaluation of druggability recovery; (3) mechanistic resolution sufficient to define expected outputs at every pipeline stage; (4) tissue specificity that exercises the pipeline's cross-program regulatory data integration; and (5) complementarity between disease categories such that the benchmark pair jointly tests internal coverage capacity and the system's ability to surface and manage external data gaps.

The selected benchmark loci and their performance-relevant characteristics are as follows.

*TCF7L2 (type 2 diabetes).* The rs7903146 locus at TCF7L2 represents the most robustly replicated common-variant T2D signal across ancestries. Functional studies have established that the risk haplotype reduces TCF7L2 expression in pancreatic beta cells, impairing WNT-pathway-dependent incretin amplification of insulin secretion. TCF7L2 is not a direct small-molecule target; its primary value as a benchmark is testing Stage 1 credible-set resolution in a pancreatic islet regulatory context and Stage 2 eQTL colocalization while acknowledging the islet eQTL gap within the twenty Common Fund programs. Recovery of TCF7L2 in this context demonstrates that the access governance module correctly identifies the external data dependency and that the pipeline appropriately flags incomplete evidence before advancing to a high-confidence causal assignment.

*SLC30A8 (type 2 diabetes).* The rs13266634 locus at SLC30A8 encodes the beta-cell-restricted zinc transporter ZnT8. The combination of a common missense variant and subsequently identified rare loss-of-function protective variants establishes SLC30A8 as a genuine causal gene with near-exclusive pancreatic islet expression. This provides a stringent Stage 1 tissue-specificity test: a pipeline that assigns the signal to a non-islet tissue or a neighboring gene has failed to capture the relevant regulatory context. At Stage 3, SLC30A8 provides a non-Tclin (Tchem) druggability benchmark complementing the approved-target benchmarks below.

*PCSK9 (LDL cholesterol).* PCSK9 represents the canonical post-GWAS drug discovery success. Loss-of-function variants identified in GWAS and family-based studies demonstrated LDL cholesterol lowering and cardiovascular risk reduction, directly motivating the development of the approved inhibitor antibodies evolocumab (Repatha) and alirocumab (Praluent). The mechanism is fully elucidated and the Tclin classification is unambiguous. Liver is directly represented in GTEx, HuBMAP, and multiple LINCS cell-line profiles, enabling fully internal Stage 1 through Stage 4 execution without external supplementation. PCSK9 constitutes the cleanest end-to-end positive control in the benchmark set.

*APOC3 (triglycerides).* APOC3 provides a second approved-target benchmark for lipid metabolic traits. Rare APOC3 loss-of-function variants lower triglycerides through relief of lipoprotein lipase inhibition and accelerated VLDL clearance. Volanesorsen (Waylivra), an antisense oligonucleotide targeting APOC3 mRNA, received regulatory approval for familial chylomicronemia syndrome, placing APOC3 at Tclin. APOC3 complements PCSK9 by testing a different therapeutic modality (oligonucleotide versus antibody) and a triglyceride-rich lipoprotein phenotype rather than LDL-centric disease.

**Primary performance metrics.** Three metrics will be evaluated against the benchmark loci:

1. *Stage 1 causal gene recovery rate:* The expected causal gene (TCF7L2, SLC30A8, PCSK9, or APOC3) must appear within the top-ranked gene list emitted by Stage 1, with a multi-modal evidence posterior in the upper decile of all candidates scored at the locus.

2. *Stage 3 approved-target identification:* For PCSK9 and APOC3, the approved drug (evolocumab/alirocumab and volanesorsen, respectively) must be identified as a top-ranked Stage 3 druggability hit, with the target carrying a Tclin classification. For SLC30A8, a Tchem or above classification must be recovered.

3. *End-to-end pipeline completion:* The full five-stage pipeline must complete without unrecoverable errors for all four benchmark loci, with all intermediate evidence written to the knowledge graph and all output artifacts deposited to the artifact store.

**Secondary performance metrics.** Secondary evaluation criteria include: colocalization H4 posterior probabilities at Stage 2 concordant with published GTEx colocalization results for liver (PCSK9, APOC3) and whole pancreas (TCF7L2, SLC30A8); TWAS association statistics at Stage 2 consistent with published S-PrediXcan results; LINCS connectivity scores at Stage 4 concordant with published connectivity analyses for PCSK9 inhibition; Stage 5 biomarker candidates overlapping established protein biomarker evidence for type 2 diabetes and dyslipidemia from published literature; pipeline output reproducibility across three independent runs with identical inputs and the same random seed; and correct access-flag identification for all controlled-access datasets required by each locus before execution begins.

### C.9 Disease Applicability Assessment

The pipeline is applicable across a broad range of common complex diseases, with applicability conditioned by the availability of relevant eQTL data, chromatin regulatory context, perturbation signatures, and clinical validation cohorts within the current dataset complement. Based on a systematic stage-by-stage evaluation, the pipeline achieves the highest end-to-end empirical support for the following disease categories.

**Type 2 diabetes and related metabolic syndrome** (overall: High). GTEx covers the metabolically relevant tissues (pancreas, liver, adipose, skeletal muscle); iHMP and Bridge2AI/AI-READI provide longitudinal multi-omic cohorts for Stage 5 biomarker modeling. The islet eQTL gap at Stage 2 requires pre-declared external supplementation but does not alter pipeline architecture.

**Lipid metabolic diseases** (overall: High). Thousands of independently associated loci with high statistical resolution provide a dense Stage 1 and Stage 2 input; liver tissue is directly represented across GTEx, HuBMAP, and multiple LINCS models; IDG contains Tclin-classified entries for HMGCR, PCSK9, LDLR, ANGPTL3, APOC3, and CETP; MoTrPAC contributes exercise-induced lipid metabolism data across liver and adipose.

**Inflammatory bowel disease** (overall: Moderate-High). Immune cell eQTL data from external resources (eQTLGen, DICE, OneK1K) supplement GTEx for Stage 2; iHMP IBDMDB provides the primary Stage 5 longitudinal multi-omic cohort with matched outcome data.

**Acute and chronic pain** (overall: Moderate-High). A2CPS constitutes the most directly applicable Stage 5 resource within the current portfolio for biomarker and treatment-response modeling specific to the acute-to-chronic pain transition.

**Aging-related diseases with senescence-relevant biology** (overall: Moderate-High). SenNet provides cell-state-stratified chromatin and transcriptomic data across senescent and non-senescent cell states for Stages 1 and 2; MoTrPAC contributes matched multi-omic time series for muscle and adipose aging contexts.

**Osteoporosis** (overall: Low-Moderate). GWAS input is well-powered, but no bone-specific eQTL data exist within the twenty Common Fund programs, constituting a structural Stage 2 gap requiring external supplementation with bone cell eQTL catalogs from the eQTL Catalogue before Stage 2 can contribute meaningfully.

### C.11 Potential Pitfalls and Alternative Approaches

**Pitfall 1: LLM hallucination in tool selection and parameter specification.** Stage agents may generate tool calls with incorrect parameters or may invoke tools in inappropriate contexts, introducing errors that propagate downstream. *Mitigation:* Structured output mode constrains all agent outputs to Pydantic-validated schemas; tool calls return typed result objects that are validated before state update; uncertainty flags are emitted and escalated to the human review layer when tool outputs fall outside expected ranges. All statistical tools are implemented as deterministic Nextflow processes invoked as subprocesses, isolating LLM reasoning from statistical computation.

**Pitfall 2: Incomplete cell-context matching in Stage 4 perturbation analysis.** The LINCS L1000 compendium is predominantly generated from cancer-derived cell lines, which may not accurately represent the tissue microenvironment relevant to non-oncologic diseases. *Mitigation:* Cell-context match scores are computed explicitly and propagated as uncertainty metadata on Stage 4 output edges. The session plan presents available cell-line coverage for the disease of interest to the investigator before execution; investigators may modify the plan to restrict Stage 4 to cell lines meeting a minimum context-match threshold. MoTrPAC and SenNet provide orthogonal perturbation references for metabolic and aging-related disease contexts, partially offsetting the cancer cell-line bias for these phenotypes.

**Pitfall 3: Controlled-access authorization failures during execution.** Retrieval failures for controlled-access datasets (GTEx via dbGaP, H3Africa via EGA, iHMP, Kids First, UDN) may interrupt pipeline execution mid-run. *Mitigation:* The access governance module consults the authorization manifest at plan generation time and surfaces all authorization gaps before execution begins. The manifest distinguishes between datasets that are required for plan execution and those that are optional supplements, allowing execution to proceed on publicly accessible resources while authorization for controlled-access supplements is obtained in parallel.

**Pitfall 4: Knowledge graph identifier collisions across programs.** Different Common Fund programs may represent the same biological entity under incompatible identifiers, leading to duplicated or incorrectly merged nodes. *Mitigation:* The harmonizer component resolves all entities to canonical ontology identifiers before any write operation and maintains a cross-reference table updated from authoritative ontology REST APIs at pipeline initialization. Merge decisions are logged in the audit layer for investigator review.

**Pitfall 5: Reproducibility degradation from stochastic LLM outputs.** LLM temperature introduces non-determinism in agent reasoning, making exact reproduction of a session difficult. *Mitigation:* Statistical computations are performed by deterministic Nextflow processes with fixed random seeds recorded in the provenance log. LLM temperature is set to zero for all structured output calls where determinism is required. Session plans, which encode the primary analytical intent, are recorded verbatim in the artifact store. The audit layer records the model identifier and parameter configuration for every agent invocation, enabling retrospective reproduction of reasoning context.

- **Reference 1**
  Claussnitzer, M., Cho, J.H., Collins, R., Cox, N.J., Dermitzakis, E.T. & Hurles, M.E. et al. A brief history of human disease genetics. *Nature* **577**, 179–189 (2020). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-019-1879-7

  **Content in document [1–4]:** "yet the causal genes, regulatory mechanisms, and druggable targets underlying the  majority of these loci remain unresolved"

  **Supporting content in source:** "Although tens of thousands of associations have been discovered across thousands of common human diseases and traits, multiple factors have frustrated efforts to convert these genetic signals to knowledge about causal variants, genes and mechanisms." The authors review how functional interpretation of non-coding variants — where the majority of GWAS signals fall — depends on connecting association signals to downstream effector genes, a step that remains incomplete for most loci.

- **Reference 2**
  Tam, V., Patel, N., Turcotte, M., Bossé, Y., Paré, G. & Meyre, D. Benefits and limitations of genome-wide association studies. *Nat. Rev. Genet.* **20**, 467–484 (2019). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41576-019-0127-1

  **Content in document [1–4]:** "yet the causal genes, regulatory mechanisms, and druggable targets underlying the  majority of these loci remain unresolved"

  **Supporting content in source:** The review documents that despite GWAS identifying thousands of disease-associated loci, "most association signals reflect variants and genes with no direct biological relevance to disease." The authors identify the translation of statistical associations into causal mechanisms as a principal unresolved challenge, noting that the field has not yet established a systematic path from genetic discovery to therapeutic application for the majority of identified loci.

- **Reference 3**
  Gallagher, M.D. & Chen-Plotkin, A.S. The post-GWAS era: from association to function. *Am. J. Hum. Genet.* **102**, 717–730 (2018). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajhg.2018.04.002

  **Content in document [1–4]:** "yet the causal genes, regulatory mechanisms, and druggable targets underlying the  majority of these loci remain unresolved"

  **Supporting content in source:** "Functional studies aimed at delineating the causal genetic variants and biological mechanisms underlying the observed statistical associations with disease risk have lagged." The authors frame the post-GWAS era as defined precisely by this gap between statistical association and functional mechanistic understanding, and review experimental strategies that have begun to address it.

- **Reference 4**
  Cannon, M.E. & Mohlke, K.L. Deciphering the emerging complexities of molecular mechanisms at GWAS loci. *Am. J. Hum. Genet.* **103**, 637–653 (2018). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajhg.2018.10.001

  **Content in document [1–4]:** "yet the causal genes, regulatory mechanisms, and druggable targets underlying the  majority of these loci remain unresolved"

  **Supporting content in source:** The review describes "the complexity of mechanisms at GWAS loci — including multiple signals, multiple variants, and/or multiple genes — and the implications these complexities hold for experimental study design and interpretation of GWAS mechanisms." The authors emphasize that despite thousands of identified SNP-trait associations, only a small fraction have been functionally investigated, and that emerging architectural complexities (allelic heterogeneity, pleiotropy, regulatory context-dependence) compound the resolution challenge.

- **Reference 5**
  Cano-Gamez, E. & Trynka, G. From GWAS to function: using functional genomics to identify the mechanisms underlying complex diseases. *Front. Genet.* **11**, 424 (2020). https://doi.org/10.3389/fgene.2020.00424

  **Content in document [5,6]:** "these resources remain isolated from GWAS workflows and from each other, accessible primarily to domain experts"

  **Supporting content in source:** "The majority of disease-associated loci lie in non-coding regions of the genome and, even though they are thought to play a role in gene expression regulation, it is unclear which genes they regulate and in which cell types or physiological contexts this regulation occurs. This has hindered the translation of GWAS findings into clinical interventions." The review demonstrates that effective post-GWAS integration requires simultaneous command of GWAS summary statistics, functional genomics datasets, colocalization methods, and tissue-specific regulatory annotations — a combination that places the analysis beyond the reach of investigators without dedicated multi-domain expertise.

- **Reference 6**
  Falola, O., Adam, Y. et al. SysBiolPGWAS: simplifying post-GWAS analysis through the use of computational technologies and integration of diverse omics datasets. *Bioinformatics* **39**, btac791 (2023). https://doi.org/10.1093/bioinformatics/btac791

  **Content in document [5,6]:** "these resources remain isolated from GWAS workflows and from each other, accessible primarily to domain experts"

  **Supporting content in source:** "It is challenging to perform pGWAS analysis for researchers with limited bioinformatics skills due to the complexity of installing some pGWAS tools, complex command line parameters, or the amount of genomic data required for the pGWAS analysis." The SysBiolPGWAS paper was developed specifically to lower this barrier; its stated motivation is that existing post-GWAS tools are largely inaccessible to biomedical researchers who lack deep computational infrastructure, directly corroborating the accessibility claim in the document.

- **Reference 7**
  Huang, J. et al. Twenty years of genome-wide association studies: health translation challenges and AI opportunities. *Eur. J. Hum. Genet.* (2025). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41431-025-01951-5

  **Content in document [7]:** "No automated, end-to-end infrastructure currently exists to orchestrate across them through a principled translational inference chain"

  **Supporting content in source:** "Despite tremendous progress, several foundational challenges persist in GWAS over the past two decades." The authors systematically review the persistent gap between genomic discovery and clinical translation — including the absence of integrated, automated analysis infrastructure — and frame AI-based approaches as the most promising path forward, identifying automated multi-dataset orchestration as an unmet need within the field.

- **Reference 8**
  Mountjoy, E., Schmidt, E.M., Carmona, M., Schwartzentruber, J., Peat, G. & Miranda, A. et al. An open approach to systematically prioritize causal variants and genes at all published human GWAS trait-associated loci. *Nat. Genet.* **53**, 1527–1533 (2021). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-021-00945-5

  **Content in document [8]:** "a unified knowledge graph enabling multi-hop evidence queries from genetic variant to therapeutic target"

  **Supporting content in source:** "Genome-wide association studies (GWASs) have identified many variants associated with complex traits, but identifying the causal gene(s) is a major challenge. In the present study, we present an open resource that provides systematic fine mapping at human GWAS loci, enabling users to prioritize genes at disease-associated regions and assess their potential as drug targets." Open Targets Genetics, the resource described in this paper, represents the most comprehensive prior approach to structured variant-to-gene evidence integration across all published GWAS loci. AgentGWAS extends this prior work by embedding a similar evidence integration strategy within an automated, multi-program orchestration pipeline rather than a static pre-computed database.

- **Reference 9**
  Chen, X. et al. Artificial intelligence agents for biological research: a survey. *Brief. Bioinform.* **27**, bbag075 (2025). https://doi.org/10.1093/bib/bbag075

  **Content in document:** "No existing tool covers all five stages within a single automated workflow"

  **Supporting content in source:** This survey reviews the landscape of AI agent frameworks applied to biological and biomedical research tasks, cataloguing existing tools by capability scope. The authors identify that current agentic systems address isolated analytical steps (literature retrieval, sequence analysis, pathway query) but none integrates the complete chain from genetic variant prioritization through druggability assessment to clinical biomarker projection within a single orchestrated workflow, directly supporting the claim that no existing tool covers the full translational pipeline.

- **Reference 10**
  Tan, X. et al. Streamline automated biomedical discoveries with agentic bioinformatics. *Brief. Bioinform.* **26**, bbaf505 (2025). https://doi.org/10.1093/bib/bbaf505

  **Content in document:** "general-purpose autonomous agent frameworks...fail to address post-GWAS translational analysis at four structural barriers"

  **Supporting content in source:** The paper characterizes the current generation of agentic bioinformatics frameworks and identifies recurring failure modes when general orchestration architectures are applied to domain-specific genomic analysis tasks. The authors note that statistical tool configuration, data access governance, and multi-step uncertainty propagation represent persistent gaps that general-purpose LLM orchestrators cannot resolve without domain-specific architectural constraints, corroborating the four structural barriers enumerated in the document.

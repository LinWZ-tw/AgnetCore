# **AgentGWAS: An Agentic AI for End-to-End Post-GWAS Translational Analysis Using NIH Common Fund Program Data**
## Background and Significance
Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified tens of thousands of robust variant-trait associations across common complex diseases, yet fewer than 10% of these signals have been mechanistically resolved to causal genes, regulatory mechanisms, or druggable targets. The translational gap arises not only from absence of data but also from the complexity of integrating heterogeneous multi-modal resources spanning chromatin architecture, tissue-specific gene expression, pharmacological perturbation databases, and longitudinal clinical cohorts. Solving these issues currently requires specialized bioinformatics expertise and months of effort each disease requires months of intervention.

The NIH Common Fund has invested substantially in generating precisely the orthogonal molecular datasets required to close this gap, through twenty programs including the 4D Nucleome (4DN), Genotype-Tissue Expression (GTEx), Human BioMolecular Atlas Program (HuBMAP), Library of Integrated Network-based Cellular Signatures (LINCS), Illuminating the Druggable Genome (IDG), and Molecular Transducers of Physical Activity in Humans (MoTrPAC), etc. Although these programs encompass genomics, epigenomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, perturbation biology, and deep phenotyping, they remain isolated from each other and GWAS workflows, accessible chiefly to experts adept at navigating diverse access levels, file formats, and identifier systems. No automated, end-to-end software infrastructure currently exists to orchestrate across these resources through a principled translational inference chain.

## Long-Term Goal, Objective, and Central Hypothesis
The long-term goal of this research is to enable any investigator with a GWAS summary statistic to obtain a prioritized, evidence-graded list of causal genes, druggable targets, and protein biomarkers without requiring specialist bioinformatics infrastructure. The **objective** of the present application is to develop, benchmark, and openly release **AgentGWAS**, a modular agentic software pipeline that autonomously orchestrates a five-stage post-GWAS workflow by integrating twenty NIH Common Fund datasets as authoritative analytical inputs: (1) **variant-to-gene** resolution, (2) **variant-to-transcriptome** propagation, (3) **druggability** assessment, (4) **in-silico perturbation** simulation, and (5) **transcriptome-to-proteome** biomarker projection. 

The **plan** is to implement a large language model-orchestrated, multi-agent pipeline that incorporates structured analytical stages, calibrated uncertainty propagation, and human-in-the-loop plan review. This design aims to significantly streamline the generation of actionable post-GWAS translational insights while maintaining methodological rigor. The approach leverages recent advances in LLM reasoning, agent orchestration frameworks, and the demonstrated capacity of multi-agent systems to execute complex, domain-specific tool chains across diverse APIs.

## Specific Aim
**Develop an agentic post-GWAS translational pipeline integrating twenty NIH Common Fund program datasets.** The pipeline will be implemented as a Python package with a Streamlit graphical interface and an AWS-deployable production configuration. A planner agent will parse natural-language analysis requests into structured, human-reviewable session plans that identify the relevant datasets, statistical thresholds, and genome build for each proposed run. Five LangGraph-orchestrated stage subagents will then execute, in sequence and with bidirectional error propagation: (Stage 1) fine-mapping and multi-modal variant-to-gene scoring <!-- integrating 4DN, HuBMAP, SenNet, and GTEx)-->; (Stage 2) eQTL colocalization and transcriptome-wide association <!--integrating GTEx, H3Africa, MoTrPAC, and SenNet-->; (Stage 3) druggability annotation <!--integrating IDG, KOMP2, and LINCS-->; (Stage 4) connectivity-based in-silico perturbation <!--integrating LINCS, Bridge2AI, and MoTrPAC--> ; and (Stage 5) transcriptome-to-proteome projection and biomarker prioritization <!--integrating MoTrPAC, A2CPS, iHMP, ExRNA, and Metabolomics Workbench-->. All intermediate results will be persisted as typed nodes and edges in a property knowledge graph <!--Neo4j locally; Amazon Neptune for cloud deployment-->, with calibrated uncertainty scores as edge properties. An access governance layer will consult a formal access manifest before invoking any controlled-access resource. Reproducibility will be enforced through structured logging of tool versions, parameter configurations, random seeds, and artifact hashes. **Validation** will employ established benchmark loci for type 2 diabetes <!--TCF7L2, SLC30A8--> and lipid metabolic traits <!--PCSK9, APOC3-->, evaluating recovery of known causal genes and approved therapeutic targets as primary performance metrics.

## Expected Outcomes and Significance
Successful completion of Aim will deliver a publicly released, documented, and benchmarked software infrastructure that any investigator can deploy — locally via Docker Compose or at scale on AWS — to conduct reproducible, end-to-end post-GWAS analysis. By providing structured programmatic interfaces to all twenty Common Fund program datasets through a unified agentic orchestration layer, AgentGWAS will substantially lower the barrier to evidence integration across these resources, convert months of manual expert effort into hours of automated analysis, and directly accelerate the translational impact of the NIH Common Fund portfolio. The open-source release, together with a benchmarked demonstration on metabolic and musculoskeletal disease loci, will establish a reusable computational infrastructure readily extensible to new Common Fund datasets and emerging analytical stages as the field advances.
